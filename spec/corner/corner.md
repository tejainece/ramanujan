# Corner Rounding — Current Implementation

Ramanujan's corner-rounding surface replaces the sharp joint between two segments with a smooth or beveled transition. This document describes what exists today; see [competitor_analysis.md](competitor_analysis.md) for how it compares to Illustrator, Inkscape, Figma, and Skia.

> **In one line:** given two segments (straight or curved) that share an endpoint, cut both back by a radius and bridge the gap with an arc, an ellipse, a bevel, or a curvature-continuous curve — available both as single-corner primitives and as a whole-path operation (`roundAllCorners`) that fillets every vertex of a `VectorPath`/`Loop` in one call.

---

## What exists

`ramanujan` exposes six corner-rounding functions, all operating on the same shape of input: two segments that meet at a shared vertex, each either a straight line or a curve (`QuadraticSegment`, `CubicSegment`, `CircularArcSegment`, or `ArcSegment`). Each cuts both sides back from the shared point (independently, by its own `radius1`/`radius2`, unless noted otherwise below) and replaces the sharp joint with a fillet — returning the leading trimmed segment, the fillet segment, and the trailing trimmed segment as a three-piece replacement for the original corner. On top of these, `roundAllCorners` rounds every corner of a whole `VectorPath` or `Loop` in one call — locating each vertex, applying the chosen style, clamping radii across corners that compete for the same run of path, and splicing everything back together (see "Whole-path rounding" below). See "Rounding a corner where an adjacent segment is a curve" below for what changes, and what doesn't quite generalize for free, when either side is curved rather than straight.

The six functions cover four corner *styles*:

- **Round** — `roundCornerUsingCircularArc` (equal-radius case, a true circle) and `roundCornerUsingEllipticArc` (independent per-side radii, an ellipse).
- **Inverted round** — `roundCornerUsingInvertedArc`, the concave counterpart of round.
- **Chamfer** — `roundCornerUsingChamfer`, a straight bevel.
- **Curvature-continuous / Bezier** — `roundCornerUsingQuadraticBezier`, `roundCornerUsingCubicBezier`, and `roundCornerUsingSquircle` (an alias of the cubic construction, exposed for its curvature-continuity property).

## Circular arc rounding

`roundCornerUsingCircularArc` is geometrically exact: cut both lines back by a radius, take the perpendicular to each line at its cut point — the fillet's center must lie on both, since a circle tangent to a line at a point has its center on that line's normal — and intersect the two perpendiculars to find the center. The arc's own radius then falls out as the distance from that center to either cut point, and the arc's direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise) is chosen from the sign of the turn between the two lines. This produces a true, radius-accurate rounded corner for any turn angle.

It accepts two radius parameters (`radius1` for the incoming side, `radius2` for the outgoing side) but averages them into a single radius before cutting either line. This is intentional, not a shortcut: a circle tangent to two lines necessarily has equal tangent length on both sides (from the shared vertex, any two tangent segments to the same circle are equal — the tangent-length theorem), so a real circle has exactly one true radius per corner. There is no way to honor two independently-chosen radii and still return a literal circle. Callers who need the two sides to differ should reach for `roundCornerUsingEllipticArc` instead, which is built for exactly that case.

`radius1` and `radius2` are each clamped to at most their own adjacent line's length before averaging — see "Radius clamping against adjacent edge length" below. This matters more than it would for an independent-radius style: because the two are averaged into one shared radius before either line is cut, an oversized `radius2` would otherwise inflate the averaged radius enough to over-cut `segment1` too, even when `radius1` on its own is perfectly reasonable. Clamping each side to its own length before averaging closes that off.

## Elliptic arc rounding

`roundCornerUsingEllipticArc` is the independent-per-side-radius counterpart to the circular arc: `radius1` cuts line 1 back and `radius2` cuts line 2 back, fully independently, and the two cut points are joined by the *unique ellipse* tangent to both lines there.

The construction works in the oblique coordinate frame whose axes run along the two input lines: in that frame the corner becomes a right angle, and the ellipse centered at `(radius1, radius2)` with semi-axes `(radius1, radius2)` is tangent to both axes exactly at the two cut points, by construction. Mapping that back to world space gives a general affine image of a circle — an ellipse whose canonical center/radii/rotation are recovered from the eigendecomposition of the resulting shape matrix. The function returns an `ArcSegment` rather than a `CircularArcSegment`.

Two things worth knowing about this construction:

- It reduces to a true circle only when the corner is a right angle *and* the two radii match. Away from 90°, a conic tangent to two given lines at two given points has one remaining degree of freedom beyond "the two radii are equal," so equal radii on a non-right-angle corner still produce a genuine (non-circular) ellipse, not the same result as `roundCornerUsingCircularArc`. Both curves are exactly tangent at the cut points either way.
- Picking which of the two possible arcs (short way vs. long way around) to draw is done by reconstructing both candidate `ArcSegment`s and comparing their derived centers against the center computed directly from the tangency construction, rather than by hand-deriving `ArcSegment`'s sweep-flag sign convention. This also sidesteps a pre-existing bug in `Ellipse.arc()`/`Ellipse.tAtAngle()`, which throws for angles that land exactly on a quadrant boundary — a common case here, since cut points frequently sit on an axis.

Like the circular arc, `radius1` and `radius2` are each clamped to at most their own adjacent line's length (see below) — here that clamp is the whole story, since the two radii are already used independently and there's no averaging step for one side's clamp to interact with.

## Inverted round rounding

`roundCornerUsingInvertedArc` is the concave counterpart to `roundCornerUsingCircularArc` — Illustrator's "Inverted Round," Inkscape's "Inverse Fillet." Both lines are cut back to exactly the same points a normal round would use (`radius1`/`radius2`, averaged into one `radius`, for the same tangent-length reason `roundCornerUsingCircularArc` averages — see above), but instead of bridging those points with an arc tangent to both lines, it bridges them with the arc of the *literal circle of that radius centered on the original vertex*. This is exactly the manual construction real design tools use for the style: draw a circle centered on the corner, then Boolean-subtract it.

Because each line passes straight through that circle's center, it meets the arc at a right angle rather than blending into it — this is a genuine notch (a picture-frame-mat/movie-ticket-corner look), not a smooth fillet, and unlike `roundCornerUsingCircularArc` it never extends past the original vertex; the arc only ever bites material away from inside the original corner angle. Like `roundCornerUsingCircularArc`, the arc's sweep direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise) is chosen from the sign of the turn between the two lines, so the notch lands centered on the vertex regardless of which way the corner turns — a corner turning either direction (e.g. a vertical edge sweeping clockwise into a horizontal one, or the mirror case) gets a centered notch, not a hook-shaped curve.

## Chamfer rounding

`roundCornerUsingChamfer` is the straight-bevel style: cut each line back independently by its own radius and connect the two cut points with a single straight line segment. Unlike every arc-based style above, a straight line carries no tangency constraint linking the two sides, so `radius1` and `radius2` are always honored exactly, however different they are — there's no geometric obstacle here the way there is for a true circle.

## Bezier rounding (quadratic and cubic) and squircle

The quadratic version cuts each line back independently by its own radius and places its single control point at the original sharp corner. This is the common "corner-cut" quadratic trick — it softens the joint, and different left/right radii do produce different-looking curves — but the result carries no real relationship between the radius values and how round the corner actually looks, and it is not a circular arc.

The cubic version also cuts each line back independently, and places *both* interior control points at the shared vertex. Three consecutive control points that are collinear force zero curvature at that end of the curve — `{p1, vertex, vertex}` and `{vertex, vertex, p2}` are each trivially collinear — so this construction has exactly zero curvature at both ends, matching the zero curvature of the straight lines it meets. It's also, provably, the *only* placement of a single cubic's two interior control points that achieves that: the only point lying on both input lines is the vertex itself, so there's no freedom to place them anywhere else and keep both ends curvature-matched. The three returned segments connect exactly, end to end.

`roundCornerUsingSquircle` is this same vertex-anchored cubic construction, exposed under its own name. It isn't a distinct algorithm from `roundCornerUsingCubicBezier` — as noted above, the vertex-anchored placement is the *only* way a single cubic gets curvature continuity at both ends, so there's nothing else to build. It's given its own entry point because the curvature-continuity property (curvature easing smoothly instead of jumping instantly at the tangent points, the way it does for a circular or elliptic arc) is what callers reaching for "squircle"/superellipse-style corners are actually asking for, and that property deserves a name that says so rather than making every caller rediscover it from the cubic's construction. It is not an exact reproduction of Figma's corner-smoothing curve or a true superellipse — those blend a circular-arc-like middle section with easing curves on each side, tunable by a smoothing percentage; this is a single, fixed, curvature-continuous cubic with no equivalent tuning parameter.

## Radius clamping against adjacent edge length

All six functions clamp `radius1` to at most `segment1`'s own arc length and `radius2` to at most `segment2`'s, via a shared private helper (`_clampRadiiToEdgeLength`) applied to the raw inputs before anything else happens with them. Without this clamp, a radius bigger than its adjacent segment would simply be asked to cut back further than that segment goes; the arc-length bisection search (`_paramAtLengthFromP1`/`_paramAtChordDistanceFromP1`) is bounded to its own segment's `t ∈ [0, 1]` regardless, so the *cut point* itself never actually leaves the segment — without the clamp it would just silently land on the segment's far endpoint instead of where the caller asked for, with the excess simply discarded.

That discard is harmless for the independent-radius styles (chamfer, elliptic arc, quadratic/cubic Bézier, squircle): each side's cut point saturates at its own far endpoint and the other side is untouched. It is not harmless for the two styles that average `radius1`/`radius2` into a single shared radius before cutting anything (`roundCornerUsingCircularArc`, `roundCornerUsingInvertedArc`): an oversized radius on a *short* side would inflate the shared average and over-cut the *other*, otherwise-reasonable side too — e.g. `radius1 = 5` on a 100-unit segment paired with `radius2 = 1000` on a 10-unit segment would average to `502.5` and devour the entire 100-unit segment, not just cut it back by something close to 5. Clamping each raw radius to its own segment's length *before* averaging avoids this: the example above instead averages `min(5, 100) = 5` with `min(1000, 10) = 10` into `7.5`, matching what a caller would reasonably expect from a `radius1 = 5` request.

This clamp is strictly per-corner: it has no visibility beyond `segment1`/`segment2`'s own lengths, so it cannot prevent two *different* corners sharing the same edge from each cutting into it independently and having their cuts overlap in the middle. That cross-corner case needs a caller that can see both corners touching an edge at once — which is exactly what `roundAllCorners` is; see its cross-corner budget under "Whole-path rounding" below.

## Whole-path rounding (`roundAllCorners`)

`roundAllCorners(path, style, {radius, radii, traverseSegments})` is the whole-shape operation every surveyed design tool actually ships (see [competitor_analysis.md](competitor_analysis.md)): walk a `VectorPath` or `Loop`, fillet each vertex with one of the styles above, and splice the results back into a single connected path. The style is chosen by the `CornerStyle` enum — one member per `roundCornerUsing*` function — rather than by passing a function reference, because the operation needs to know more about a style than its call signature: `CornerStyle.averagesRadii` marks the two styles (`circularArc`, `invertedArc`) whose cut demand on *each* side of a corner is the averaged radius rather than the per-side value, and the cross-corner budget below has to reserve space for what will actually be cut.

**Corners and radii.** A corner is a junction between consecutive segments: junction `j` is where `segments[j]` ends, an open path has `numSegments - 1` junctions, and a closed path adds the wrap-around junction where the last segment meets the first. Exactly one of `radius` (one value everywhere) or `radii` (one value per junction — Figma-style per-vertex radii across the whole shape) must be given. A junction with a zero radius is left sharp, and a *smooth* junction — incoming and outgoing tangents already agreeing — is always left alone, since there is no corner there to round; with traversal enabled, smooth junctions are exactly what a large fillet cuts across. Per-corner radii are symmetric (one value per vertex, applied to both sides), matching every surveyed tool; genuinely per-side asymmetric radii remain the single-corner functions' territory.

**Cross-corner radius clamping.** Radii are clamped in two steps. First each corner's radius is capped per side at the length of the run of path that side's cut is allowed to roam (its adjacent segment, or with traversal the whole stretch to the next rounded corner — see below); this is the whole-path generalization of the per-corner clamp above, and subsumes it. Then every run is checked against the combined demand of the two corners cutting into it from either end — the classic short-edge-between-two-rounded-corners case — and when they oversubscribe it, both corners are scaled down proportionally (`run length / combined demand`; for two equal radii this reproduces the familiar "half the shorter edge" cap design tools apply). A corner squeezed on one side is scaled *as a whole* — both of its radii shrink by its worst run's factor — so the fillet keeps its proportions instead of going lopsided, and, for the averaging styles, so the averaged radius provably stays within each run's allocation (scaling the two inputs by one factor scales their average by it; scaling them by different per-side factors would not).

**Traversal (`traverseSegments`).** Off by default, matching Illustrator/Inkscape/Figma: a fillet's endpoints stay on the two segments touching the vertex, and an oversized radius clamps. When enabled, a cut that runs off the end of its adjacent segment continues into the segments beyond, consuming intermediate junctions whole — the natural behavior when a path's "sides" are really chains of several segments, such as a polyline approximating a curve. Internally both modes are the same machinery: the path is split at *barriers* (every junction when traversal is off; only the rounded corners themselves when it's on) into *stretches*, the maximal runs a cut may roam, and each stretch's length is what the per-side cap and the cross-corner budget above are computed against. A cut never crosses a rounded corner in either mode — the stretch between two consecutive rounded corners is precisely the run the two share under the budget. On a closed path with a single rounded corner the whole loop is one stretch with that corner at both ends, and the budget degenerates to the corner's two sides sharing the loop's own length.

**Execution and splicing.** Corners are processed in path order, always cutting the *surviving* geometry — each stretch is shared by at most the two corner-sides cutting its opposite ends, and the budget guarantees they fit, so sequential processing cannot produce overlapping cuts. The style constructions are the same code the single-corner functions run: `corner.dart`'s cut/fillet machinery is built from chain-aware internals (`_cutChainIncoming`/`_cutChainOutgoing` and per-style cores like `_roundChainCircular`), and the six public functions are thin wrappers around them passing single-segment chains — so the single-corner test suite guards the whole-path geometry too. The circular-arc style's tangency solve walks the outgoing chain segment by segment from the corner outward and takes the first tangency; when a candidate circle's tangent point lands exactly on (or past) the chain's trimmed end, the chain's far endpoint stands in, which both makes the boundary case findable by the sampling search and turns "chain too short" into saturation instead of failure. Output segments consumed whole by a cut are dropped, as are the zero-length leftovers of an edge exactly used up; a closed input returns a `Loop` (joins are bitwise exact by construction — fillets are anchored at the kept pieces' own endpoints, not at separately-evaluated `lerp(t)` points, precisely so `Loop`'s exact-equality closure check holds — with a final seam snap covering the one case dropping a consumed edge leaves the seam a few ulps open), and an open input's two endpoints are never moved.

**Caveats.** Two overrun caveats are inherited from the single-corner constructions and only reachable through curved geometry: the circular arc's far cut point is solved for tangency rather than prescribed, and the inverted arc's cut points are at a straight-line (chord) distance from the vertex rather than an arc-length one — so on curved sides either can consume slightly more arc length than the budget reserved. Because corners are processed in order on surviving geometry, an overrun shrinks a neighbor's fillet rather than producing overlapping or discontinuous output.

## Rounding a corner where an adjacent segment is a curve

All six functions accept any `Segment` on either side — a straight line or a quadratic, cubic, circular-arc, or elliptic-arc segment — not just `LineSegment`. This generalizes two things every function gets for free only when both sides are straight lines.

First, cutting back "by radius" has no closed form on a curve. For a line, the point at arc-length distance `r` from an endpoint is `lerp(r / length)`. For a curve there is no equivalent formula, so the parameter is found by bisection instead — sampling `Segment.bifurcateAtInterval` until the trimmed piece's own `length` matches `r`. This is exact for a line and numerically converged (not exact) for a curve, mirroring how this library already treats curve length itself (adaptive subdivision, not a formula) for quadratics and cubics.

Second, the "vertex" several constructions anchor to is really "where the two tangent lines meet." For two straight lines those are the same point, since a line's tangent is the line itself everywhere. `roundCornerUsingEllipticArc`'s oblique frame and the quadratic/cubic Béziers' control-point placement both anchor to the intersection of the two cut points' actual tangent lines rather than the shared endpoint — which reduces to the original vertex exactly when both sides are lines, and to some other point when either side curves.

`roundCornerUsingCircularArc` needs a different construction from the other five, because the line-only shortcut of cutting both sides back by the same distance and intersecting the two perpendiculars at those points only lands on a point equidistant from both cut points when the two sides are mirror-symmetric about the corner's bisector — true for two straight lines cut by *equal* amounts (which is why `radius1`/`radius2` are averaged in the first place), but not for two straight lines cut by *unequal* amounts, and with no analogous symmetry once either side is curved. Instead, only the incoming segment's cut point is prescribed by arc length; the outgoing segment's cut point and the fillet's true radius are solved for together, by searching along the incoming segment's normal line at its cut point (a necessary condition for tangency, true for any segment type) for the point where a circle centered there is also exactly tangent to the outgoing segment (found via a dense-sample-then-bisect search for the point on that segment closest to a candidate center — the standard "closest point on a curve" condition, which coincides with the tangency condition). This reduces to the original closed-form result exactly when both sides are lines.

By contrast, `roundCornerUsingEllipticArc` (an ellipse has enough degrees of freedom to be tangent to two arbitrary lines at two arbitrary given points, with no symmetry required), `roundCornerUsingInvertedArc` (any point at a given Euclidean distance from a fixed center trivially lies on a circle of that radius, regardless of curve shape), and the quadratic/cubic Bézier constructions (a Bézier's endpoint tangent is definitionally toward/from its control point, not something requiring symmetry to hold) generalize to curved sides without this complication.

Two honest caveats on what "tangent" means once a curve is involved. Bézier/squircle curvature continuity is conditional: the cubic and squircle constructions still place both interior control points at the anchor point, giving exactly zero curvature at both ends, but that only matches the adjacent segment's curvature when the adjacent segment is also a straight line (also zero curvature) — against a curved neighbor, the tangent direction still matches exactly (G1), but the curvature does not (a jump from the neighbor's actual curvature down to zero, not the tangent jump the same construction eliminates against a line); matching curvature as well would require solving for the control point from the neighbor's actual curvature at the cut point, not just its tangent, a materially heavier construction not attempted here. And all the numeric root-finding assumes a "gentle," single-corner curve — arc length and chord distance growing monotonically away from the vertex, and a single tangency point in range — which matches the scope every function here already had (a single hand-picked corner, not an arbitrary path), so it isn't a new restriction so much as the same one, now made explicit because curves can violate it in ways a line cannot.

See `test/rounded/rounded_test.dart`'s "rounding a corner where an adjacent segment is a curve" group for coverage: a circular-arc-into-line corner (one curved side, one straight) exercised against all six styles, and a cubic-into-quadratic corner (two curved sides) exercised against all six.

## Scope limitations

- **One-shot geometry replacement, not a live effect.** `roundAllCorners` (like the single-corner functions it orchestrates) bakes the fillets into real segments. There is no non-destructive representation that remembers the original sharp vertices and re-derives the fillets when a radius changes — the equivalent of Illustrator's Live Corners staying editable until Expand Appearance, or Inkscape's Corners LPE until flattening.
- **Per-corner radii are symmetric in the whole-path operation.** `roundAllCorners`' `radii` list takes one value per vertex, applied to both sides of that corner — the same surface every surveyed tool offers. Genuinely per-side asymmetric radii on one corner are available only through the single-corner functions (`roundCornerUsingEllipticArc` et al.).

## Related but distinct: offset-join rounding

The inset/outset offsetting operation (documented separately) has its own, unrelated notion of "rounding a corner": an offset-join style (miter, round, or bevel) controls how the *gap opened at a convex corner* is bridged when a path is offset outward or inward — this is the same concept as SVG's stroke-linejoin, and it is implemented correctly, including a true circular arc for the round style and a miter-limit fallback to bevel. It should not be confused with rounding the corners of the *original* path: offsetting changes the path's overall size by the offset distance, while corner rounding is meant to soften vertices while leaving the path's overall size alone.

## Related but distinct: corner detection in curve fitting

The curve-fitting feature (documented separately) has a "corner pre-pass" that *detects* corners in a raw point sequence — a sharp change in tangent direction between consecutive samples forces a split point before curve fitting runs. This is corner detection for segmentation purposes only; it has no radius parameter and produces no fillet.

## Test coverage

`test/rounded/rounded_test.dart` covers all six functions: tangency to both input lines, swept across a range of corner angles and (for the elliptic arc) radius ratios; end-to-end connectivity of the three returned segments; the elliptic arc's reduction to a true circle at a right angle; the inverted arc's cut points (matching a normal round's), its arc staying centered exactly on the original vertex across corner angles turning *both* ways, and its non-tangent right-angle meeting with each line; the cubic/squircle's zero curvature at both ends; in the "rounding a corner where an adjacent segment is a curve" group, all six functions exercised against a curved-into-straight corner and a curved-into-curved corner; and, in the "automatic radius clamping vs. adjacent edge length" group, the independent-radius styles clamping one oversized side without disturbing the other, plus the circular-arc and inverted-arc styles clamping each side to its own length *before* averaging so an oversized radius on a short side can't inflate the shared radius and over-cut a longer, otherwise-untouched side.

`test/rounded/round_all_corners_test.dart` covers the whole-path operation: every style producing a connected, closed `Loop` from a square; the cross-corner budget splitting an oversubscribed short edge proportionally (including the fully-consumed-edge case where the edge drops out of the output entirely), the averaging styles landing on the half-edge cap, and clamping-without-traversal saturating at the adjacent segment; traversal cutting across a smooth junction, swallowing an un-rounded sharp corner whole, still never crossing a *rounded* corner, and handling the single-rounded-corner closed path whose one stretch wraps the whole loop; per-vertex `radii` lists (zero entries leaving vertices sharp, differing values honoured per corner); and argument validation plus the degenerate inputs (all-smooth paths, all-zero radii, open-path endpoints staying fixed).

## Summary of gaps

| Capability | Status |
|---|---|
| Round a line–line corner with a true circular arc | Present and geometrically exact |
| ...with independently-honoured asymmetric radii | Present, as an ellipse (`roundCornerUsingEllipticArc`) — a true circle can't do this, see above |
| Round a line–line corner with a Bezier approximation | Present; quadratic and cubic both work and are continuous |
| Chamfer (straight bevel) corner style | Present (`roundCornerUsingChamfer`) |
| Inverse/concave rounding | Present (`roundCornerUsingInvertedArc`) |
| Continuous-curvature ("squircle") corner style | Present, as a single curvature-continuous cubic (`roundCornerUsingSquircle`) — not a tunable superellipse blend |
| Round a corner where either side is a curve segment | Present for all six styles — see "Rounding a corner where an adjacent segment is a curve" above for the tangent-continuity (G1) caveat on the Bezier/squircle styles against a curved neighbor |
| Round every corner of a whole path in one call | Present (`roundAllCorners`, all seven `CornerStyle`s, per-vertex radii, open and closed paths) |
| Clamp a single corner's own radius to its own adjacent edge length | Present for all six functions — see "Radius clamping against adjacent edge length" above |
| Clamp two adjacent corners' radii against a shared edge to avoid overlapping fillets | Present in `roundAllCorners` — proportional scale-down of both corners, "half the shorter edge" in the equal-radius case |
| Let a fillet's endpoint traverse past intermediate un-rounded junctions | Present, opt-in (`traverseSegments: true`) — beyond what the surveyed design tools offer |
| Non-destructive / re-editable rounding | Missing — one-shot geometry replacement (see Scope limitations) |
| Test coverage | Present (`test/rounded/rounded_test.dart`, `test/rounded/round_all_corners_test.dart`) |
